BLOG: Slow reader? Your Child May Have Dyslexia

1. Speech Delay Issues: Understanding the Signs

Dyslexia doesn’t just impact reading and writing—it can also affect how a child processes and produces spoken language.

For example, a child might mistakenly say "kepala" instead of "kelapa," due to difficulty distinguishing similar-sounding words.

2. Dyslexia Makes Reading Difficult for Children

Children with dyslexia may describe letters as if they are "moving" on the paper. This makes traditional reading methods, such as phonics, less effective.

3. Writing Can Be a Challenge for Children with Dyslexia

Children often confuse letters like "d" and "b," or numbers like "6" and "9." This highlights the need for patient, tailored teaching strategies.

4. Careless Traits

Children with dyslexia may trip or fall easily, wear shoes on the wrong feet, or have trouble identifying their dominant hand.

5. Difficulty Understanding Instructions

Dyslexic children often struggle with multi-step directions or spatial concepts like left/right. Visual aids can make instructions easier to follow.

6. Struggles with Forgetfulness

Short-term memory issues can make it hard for dyslexic children to retain lessons, leading to frustration and low self-esteem.

7. Challenges with Learning Mathematics

Grasping mathematical concepts, recalling sequences, or reading a clock can feel overwhelming for a child with dyslexia.

8. Creative Talents Shine Bright

Children with dyslexia often excel in creative and hands-on activities like painting, acting, or designing.
